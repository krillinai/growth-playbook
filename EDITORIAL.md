# Editorial & Evidence Standards

**[English](EDITORIAL.md)｜[简体中文](EDITORIAL.zh.md)**

These standards keep Growth Playbook useful as a decision system rather than a collection of famous stories. Every page should make clear what is known, who said it, what the evidence supports, and where transfer becomes unsafe.

## Editorial Principles

1. Start with a growth decision, not a personality, tactic, or company outcome.
2. Keep definitions and operating methods in knowledge modules; keep company evidence in the Case Library.
3. Prefer a narrower supported conclusion over a broad memorable claim.
4. Preserve contradictory evidence, missing data, time period, market, product, and participant perspective.
5. Do not attribute company growth to one person, framework, feature, positioning choice, or channel.
6. Treat metrics, thresholds, benchmarks, and company-reported results as context-dependent.
7. Use direct language. Avoid editorial filler such as “these sources,” “these videos,” or arguments for why an index deserves to exist.

## Source Priority

| Priority | Source type | Use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| P1 | Original data, experiment record, audited filing, research paper, official technical or policy documentation | Strongest available support for definitions, measurements, and documented outcomes |
| P2 | First-person article, book, talk, interview, or contemporary company document from a direct participant | Participant experience, methods, decisions, and stated results with explicit perspective limits |
| P3 | High-quality reporting, biography, encyclopedia, analysis, or secondary dataset with transparent sourcing | Corroboration, chronology, and context when primary evidence is incomplete |
| P4 | Aggregator, clip, unsourced summary, promotional list, or third-party retelling | Discovery only; do not use as the sole support for a consequential claim |

Prefer the earliest complete source over a clip or repost. Record publication dates when time changes the meaning of a claim. Link to the exact article, talk, filing, or document rather than a search page.

## Evidence Labels

Use these distinctions in prose even when they are not rendered as badges:

| Label | Meaning | Required phrasing |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Verified fact | Supported by a reliable record and not materially disputed | State the fact and cite the record |
| Participant account | A person describes their own role, decision, method, or result | Name the participant and identify the account as retrospective when relevant |
| Company claim | A company reports a metric, outcome, customer count, or capability | Attribute it to the company and avoid independent confirmation language |
| Editorial synthesis | The Playbook connects multiple records into a model or comparison | Mark the connection as a reconstruction, interpretation, or supported conclusion |
| Causal conclusion | An intervention caused a measured outcome | Require experiment or strong causal design details; otherwise use hypothesis or association language |

Words such as “drove,” “caused,” “proved,” and “led to” require causal support. When evidence is observational, use “was associated with,” “is consistent with,” “supports investigating,” or “the participant reports.”

## Minimum Page Schemas

### Person Page

- identity and growth-relevant career context;
- `Apply This Lens` link when a person-specific skill exists;
- methods or frameworks tied to decisions and evidence;
- dated article, talk, interview, or book index;
- related companies, knowledge modules, and themes;
- limitations, attribution boundaries, and source list.

Do not include pronouns, unrelated biography, or current-role language that cannot be maintained reliably.

### Company Page

- company and product context for the period covered;
- growth problems, mechanisms, metrics, and decision boundaries;
- participant perspectives clearly attributed;
- company-specific evidence before cross-company interpretation;
- related people, themes, and knowledge modules;
- source list and evidence limitations.

A company page can contain multiple cases, but it should not become a general company history.

### Knowledge Module

- definitions and boundaries;
- model, components, or decision logic;
- metric definitions and diagnostic method where relevant;
- operating guidance, common mistakes, and maturity or governance where useful;
- related Case Library evidence;
- evidence limits and conditions for revalidation.

Knowledge modules explain concepts. They should not use one company story as the definition of a concept.

### Theme Page

- an evidence matrix;
- exactly five detailed company cases;
- supported conclusion and missing evidence for every case;
- cross-company patterns;
- transfer matrix;
- concept evidence map linking to the knowledge module;
- related people and evidence boundaries.

Theme pages compare evidence. Definitions, metric trees, diagnostic protocols, and maturity models belong in knowledge modules.

## Quantitative Claims

For a metric or reported lift, preserve as many of these as available:

- numerator, denominator, unit, and segment;
- start and end state;
- observation and maturation window;
- baseline and comparison;
- absolute and relative change;
- uncertainty or sample size;
- attribution method and incrementality;
- guardrails, costs, and downstream retention;
- source date and whether the number is official, participant-reported, or estimated.

Do not convert approximate figures into precise figures. Do not compare metrics with different definitions as if they were equivalent.

## Translation and Indexing

- Publish only English and Simplified Chinese repository pages.
- Keep paired pages structurally equivalent and source lists aligned.
- Put links directly on person and company names instead of separate `Page` columns.
- Show the total when an index uses `View all` or `查看更多`.
- Add reciprocal links among knowledge, theme, company, and person pages when the relationship is substantive.
- Keep stable paths even when public labels change.

## Review Standard

A reviewer should be able to answer:

1. Which decision becomes clearer?
2. What is the strongest evidence?
3. Which statement is participant or company perspective rather than verified fact?
4. What evidence is missing?
5. Where would direct transfer fail?
6. Are the English and Chinese pages equivalent?
7. Are indexes, totals, links, and anchors accurate?
