## Where It Fits

```text
Market and competitor research
-> search and topic hypotheses
-> technical and content diagnosis
-> prioritized execution
-> visibility monitoring
-> first-party activation, retention, and revenue validation
```

Semrush is most useful before and around execution: understanding discoverable demand, comparing public competitor signals, finding technical or content gaps, and monitoring visibility. It cannot determine Product-Market Fit or prove that a ranking, mention, or click caused retained growth.

## Core Jobs

### 1. Search Demand and Keyword Research

- Discover queries, topics, intent patterns, estimated demand, and competitive difficulty.
- Group terms by customer problem, journey stage, and intended page rather than treating every keyword as an isolated target.
- Compare branded and non-branded demand to understand whether acquisition depends on existing awareness.

### 2. Competitor and Market Research

- Compare public search visibility, topics, paid terms, backlinks, and content patterns.
- Identify gaps and hypotheses, not a list of pages to copy.
- Distinguish direct product competitors from publishers, marketplaces, communities, and aggregators competing for the same query.

### 3. Technical Site Auditing

- Surface crawlability, indexing, internal-linking, metadata, performance, and other technical issues exposed by the platform.
- Prioritize issues by affected important pages and likely customer impact rather than raw warning count.
- Confirm findings with search-engine tools, server logs, browser testing, and engineering investigation where necessary.

### 4. Backlink and Authority Research

- Study referring domains, link destinations, anchor patterns, and competitor authority sources.
- Use the research to identify credible publications, communities, partners, datasets, and resources that may deserve outreach or original work.
- Do not treat link quantity as authority or pursue manipulative acquisition.

### 5. AI Search and Brand Visibility

- Monitor how a brand, topic, or competitor appears across supported AI-search surfaces where the product provides coverage.
- Compare mentions, citations, prompts, and source patterns with the GEO measurement framework.
- Preserve prompt sets, dates, markets, and model or product context because AI answers change over time.

### 6. Paid Search and Social Research

- Review estimated paid terms, creative patterns, audience or topic signals, and competitor activity.
- Use these signals to form tests for promise, audience, landing path, and economics.
- Validate with advertising-platform experiments, holdouts where feasible, and retained cohort economics.

## Measurement Map

| Tool signal | Useful question | Required validation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Estimated search volume | Is discoverable demand plausibly large enough to investigate? | Search-console impressions, qualified visits, customer research |
| Ranking or visibility | Is the site becoming easier to discover for the target topic? | Incremental traffic, activation, retention, assisted conversion |
| Competitor traffic estimate | Which competitors or topics deserve investigation? | Public evidence and first-party market research |
| Site-audit issue | Which technical constraint may block discovery or experience? | Search-engine diagnostics, logs, browser and engineering checks |
| Backlink signal | Which sources appear to confer discovery or authority? | Link relevance, referral quality, citations, brand trust |
| AI mention or citation | Where and how does the brand appear in supported AI answers? | Repeated prompt sampling, source inspection, downstream behavior |
| Paid keyword or creative | Which audience and promise may be worth testing? | Incremental conversion, CAC, payback, retained quality |

## Practical Workflow

1. Define the audience, problem, market, channel, and business outcome.
2. Create a stable project, competitor set, geography, device, and topic taxonomy.
3. Establish a baseline for visibility, technical issues, content coverage, authority, and paid activity.
4. Convert findings into prioritized hypotheses with an owner, expected mechanism, metric, and guardrail.
5. Execute changes in content, technical systems, distribution, or campaigns.
6. Monitor visibility changes without treating them as business outcomes.
7. Validate through first-party acquisition, activation, retention, revenue, and incrementality data.

## Limits

- Search volume, traffic, competitor, authority, and cost values are third-party estimates whose coverage and methodology can differ by market and product.
- A detected issue, keyword gap, backlink, ranking, or AI mention does not prove causal business impact.
- Features, databases, naming, limits, and pricing can change; check current official documentation.
- Access to data does not replace positioning, customer value, editorial judgment, engineering prioritization, or experimentation.
- Follow search-engine policies, platform terms, privacy requirements, and applicable law.
