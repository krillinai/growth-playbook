# Growth Model Cases

## Case 1: Facebook - Connect Social Value to Retention and Expansion

**Context.** Facebook needed to expand beyond dense early networks while preserving the social value that made people return.

**Evidence retained.** Chamath Palihapitiya, Alex Schultz, and Naomi Gleit connect real social connections, friend discovery, activation proxies, cohort retention, growth accounting, instrumentation, experiments, and internationalization. Their accounts represent participant perspectives rather than a complete company history.

```text
relevant social connections
-> early network value
-> repeated communication and content
-> retained active people
-> word of mouth, learning, and expansion
-> more reachable networks
```

**Growth-model conclusion supported.** Early behavior becomes useful when it is explicitly connected to retained value and operating decisions. Instrumentation and experimentation strengthen the learning cycle, while internationalization removes barriers after core value is visible.

**Missing evidence and transfer boundary.** Friend thresholds sat on smooth retention curves and do not prove causation. Facebook's identity network, scale, data, capital, timing, and organization are unusual, and growth cannot be attributed to one person, team, or metric.

## Case 2: HubSpot - Rebuild the Model When the Market Changes

**Context.** HubSpot Sales began with a low-friction product for individual professionals, supported by a free tier, low-priced subscription, virality, and paid acquisition. Retention evidence did not guarantee a path to the intended business scale.

**Evidence retained.** Brian Balfour describes Four Fits, flattening retention, market-size arithmetic, Channel-Model Fit, a pricing tier that sat between viable acquisition motions, and a later choice to focus on HubSpot's mid-market customer with coordinated product, price, channel, sales, and contract changes.

```text
defined market and problem
-> low-friction product value
-> viral and paid distribution
-> retained use and revenue
-> market and economic constraint
-> revised customer, product, channel, and model
```

**Growth-model conclusion supported.** A model must be rebuilt when the target market or route to market changes. Product retention, price, channel cost, market capacity, sales motion, and revenue model are interdependent assumptions.

**Missing evidence and transfer boundary.** The record does not contain complete market research, pricing experiments, retained economics, sales data, or a counterfactual path. HubSpot's later performance cannot be assigned to a single framework or repositioning decision.

## Case 3: Dropbox - Align Distribution With Product Value

**Context.** Dropbox's early product made file synchronization and storage useful across devices. Sharing and storage rewards could create distribution because the product output and incentive were related to that value.

**Evidence retained.** Sean Ellis discusses must-have value, activation, retention, and referral. Elena Verna connects product use to acquisition and expansion. Andrew Chen contrasts product-aligned distribution with unattractive early paid-search economics. Morgan Brown adds later evidence about Dropbox Dash and explicit AI-product evaluation.

```text
useful file or work outcome
-> sharing, collaboration, or storage need
-> qualified recipient or teammate
-> activated and retained use
-> referral, team expansion, or revenue
-> product and distribution learning
```

**Growth-model conclusion supported.** A product-led loop closes only when an output creates a qualified next input and the recipient reaches retained value. Paid and referral channels should be compared through marginal economics and retained quality, not ideology.

**Missing evidence and transfer boundary.** Evidence spans early Dropbox, later product-led work, and the Dash era. Public examples simplify referral incrementality, abuse, cohort quality, expansion authority, privacy, and AI evaluation, so they cannot form one verified causal sequence.

## Case 4: Duolingo - Join Access, Habit, Revenue, and Reinvestment

**Context.** Duolingo's model combines a free learning product, repeated practice, subscriptions, advertising, international reach, brand distribution, and ongoing product experimentation.

**Evidence retained.** Luis von Ahn connects educational access, Freemium, motivation, subscriptions, advertising, brand, and product investment. Gina Gotthilf, Albert Cheng, and Jackson Shuttleworth add evidence on organic reach, internationalization, subscription levers, explore-exploit allocation, retention, resurrection, streaks, and experiments.

```text
accessible learning value
-> broad organic and international reach
-> repeated practice and retained habit
-> subscription or advertising value
-> experimentation and product reinvestment
-> improved reach and learning experience
```

**Growth-model conclusion supported.** Free access, habit, monetization, and brand can reinforce one another when they remain constrained by repeated learning value. Revenue can fund the next product cycle, but aggressive monetization can weaken access or trust.

**Missing evidence and transfer boundary.** Public evidence does not isolate learning outcomes, brand, notifications, streaks, pricing, advertising, or market expansion. Consumer education behavior and Duolingo's brand permission do not transfer automatically to other products.

## Case 5: Uber - Build Local Cross-Side Liquidity

**Context.** Uber's customer value depended on relevant drivers and riders meeting in the same geography and time window. Total marketplace registrations did not ensure a reliable trip.

**Evidence retained.** Andrew Chen describes driver supply, pickup time, coverage, fulfillment, utilization, incentives, atomic networks, city expansion, and marketplace economics. His articles also explain why lower-frequency “Uber for X” categories could fail to reproduce rideshare supply economics.

```text
relevant driver availability
-> shorter pickup and better fulfillment
-> rider value and repeat demand
-> utilization and earning opportunity
-> retained supply and local expansion
```

**Growth-model conclusion supported.** A marketplace model must be defined at the local unit where participants receive value. Each cross-side transition requires quality, retention, incrementality, and economics; aggregate scale cannot prove a network effect.

**Missing evidence and transfer boundary.** The sources do not provide complete city cohorts, incentive holdouts, contribution data, safety outcomes, or regulatory effects. The hard side and active constraint can change across markets and periods.

## Cross-Company Patterns

### Value and retention anchor the model

Facebook used social connections as a proxy for retained social value; HubSpot used flattening retention as one fit signal; Dropbox depended on useful synchronization and sharing; Duolingo depended on repeated learning; Uber depended on reliable local transactions. Distribution without retained value does not close the model.

### Outputs create different next inputs

Facebook can create social invitations and learning, HubSpot can reinvest revenue and channel knowledge, Dropbox can create recipients and team adoption, Duolingo can reinvest subscription and advertising value, and Uber can improve cross-side density. Not every output is a loop, and each transition needs evidence.

### Economics can reverse a promising mechanism

HubSpot's pricing and channel fit, Dropbox's early paid-search comparison, Duolingo's access and monetization tradeoff, and Uber's incentive and utilization economics show that volume does not establish a scalable model.

### Models change with the unit of growth

The relevant unit can be an active person, account, team, market tier, learner, subscription, city, rider, or driver. Changing that unit changes eligibility, value, channel, retention, monetization, and expansion assumptions.

## Transfer Matrix

| Product condition | Most relevant case | What to borrow | What not to assume |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer network needs a clear activation model | Facebook | Derive early proxies from retained behavior and invest in instrumentation and learning | A famous threshold or MAU is the right metric |
| SaaS product has fit but weak scale economics | HubSpot | Test product, market, channel, price, sales motion, and market capacity together | A pricing change or move upmarket can be isolated |
| Product usage can expose or invite others | Dropbox | Measure recipient value, activation, retention, reward cost, and expansion quality | Sharing automatically creates incremental virality |
| Freemium consumer product needs sustainable revenue | Duolingo | Connect access, repeated value, subscription, brand, and reinvestment with learning guardrails | More engagement or organic attention proves durable value |
| Marketplace needs to solve cold start | Uber | Define the atomic network, hard side, local liquidity, quality, and participant economics | Aggregate supply or permanent incentives create network effects |

## Evidence and Limits

- The models are editorial reconstructions from participant accounts, articles, talks, and company pages, not verified internal system diagrams.
- The five cases span different periods, products, market structures, and outcome definitions; arrows represent hypotheses unless stronger evidence is stated.
- Company outcomes also reflect product quality, capital, market timing, competition, regulation, operations, and teams not represented here.
- A model helps expose assumptions and select evidence; it does not prove the assumptions by being coherent.
- Inclusion records transferable evidence and does not imply endorsement by a company or participant.
