## Case 1: Airtable - Intent-Based Paths for a Flexible Product

**Context.** Airtable can support many workflows. The same product flexibility that creates value also makes one generic onboarding path weak: users arrive with different goals, experience, starting assets, and preferred learning modes.

**Evidence to retain.**

    Retained super-user workflows
    -> jobs, language, and starting conditions
    -> short intent questions
    -> relevant template or guided path
    -> first useful workflow
    -> later collaboration and retention

Participant accounts connect customer-success feedback and super-user research to positioning, templates, and segmented onboarding. Questions are useful only when answers change the recommended path and improve downstream value.

**Boundary.** The reported onboarding result does not provide complete segment definitions, assignment rules, sample sizes, confidence intervals, or long-term effects by path. Collaboration can indicate deeper adoption but is not the correct outcome for every solo workflow.

## Case 2: HubSpot - Segment Choice Changes the Whole Growth System

**Context.** HubSpot Sales initially focused on individual sales contributors with a low-friction freemium model. Retained value existed, but market arithmetic and channel economics constrained the intended scale.

**Evidence to retain.**

    Individual sales users
    -> repeated email-tracking value
    -> adjacent relationship-focused professionals
    -> market and willingness-to-pay evidence
    -> broad low-ARPU path or HubSpot mid-market path
    -> coordinated product, price, channel, sales, and contract changes

Balfour's account distinguishes a user role from an account-level target market. Selecting HubSpot's established mid-market customer required more than changing a firmographic label; the product, package, acquisition motion, sales process, and economics changed together.

**Boundary.** The evidence is a retrospective account with approximate figures from 2014 to 2016. Complete segment cohorts, customer research samples, conversion, service cost, and counterfactual outcomes are unavailable.

## Case 3: Lovable - Separating ICP Signal From Hobbyist Noise

**Context.** AI products can attract broad curiosity, experimentation, and social attention. These signals can overwhelm evidence from customers who have a repeated problem, organizational intent, willingness to pay, and a path to durable use.

**Evidence to retain.**

    Broad AI interest
    -> product and account behavior
    -> repeated valuable workflow
    -> business intent and readiness
    -> Product-Led Sales or self-serve path
    -> retained use and monetization

Verna's participant perspective emphasizes separating ICP signals from hobbyist noise and connecting product behavior to activation, retention, monetization, and sales. Public proof and earned distribution should attract relevant demand rather than optimize attention alone.

**Boundary.** Public material does not disclose the full ICP definition, account qualification, behavioral thresholds, routing rules, PLS conversion, retention by segment, or cost to serve. Hobbyists may still create product learning or distribution value and should not be treated as uniformly low quality.

## Case 4: ByteDance - Market, Device, and Portfolio Conditions

**Context.** Consumer products operating across countries and a product portfolio encounter different devices, channels, payment conditions, regulation, competition, price sensitivity, and user motivations.

**Evidence to retain.**

    Market and device conditions
    -> target segment and constrained behavior
    -> main product, Lite product, or localized execution
    -> eligibility, creative, channel, and incentive differences
    -> retained value and economics
    -> portfolio cannibalization and risk checks

Xu Hongliang's account describes local teams adapting priorities and execution, Lite products reaching lower-spec-device or price-sensitive groups, and audience isolation reducing subsidized migration from a main product. A separate path is justified only when customer conditions and experience differ enough to offset duplication.

**Boundary.** The evidence spans products, markets, and years without complete segment rules, local cohorts, costs, or controlled comparisons. Device, income, and geography can become sensitive proxies; segmentation requires privacy, fairness, and regulatory review.

## Case 5: Duolingo - Consumer Segmentation by Market, State, and Intent

**Context.** Duolingo combines broad free access, international reach, learning behavior, subscriptions, retention, resurrection, and advertising. One company-level conversion or activity number can hide different customer states and market conditions.

**Evidence to retain.**

    Market + channel + learning intent + product state
    -> qualified acquisition and activation
    -> retained learning behavior
    -> free, paid, retained, resurrected, or churned state
    -> subscription value and service economics

The company case recommends segmenting acquisition, activation, conversion, retention, and revenue by market, channel, free-paid state, and learning intent. Internationalization also requires local demand, culture, payment, device, and operating evidence beyond translation.

**Boundary.** The case is an editorial synthesis of participant accounts across periods and functions. Complete segment definitions, local cohorts, learning outcomes, paywall treatment effects, attribution, and subscription economics are unavailable.

## Cross-Company Patterns

### A Segment Must Change a Decision

Airtable changes onboarding, HubSpot changes the full market and GTM system, Lovable changes qualification and sales routing, ByteDance changes product and local execution, and Duolingo changes market, lifecycle, and monetization treatment.

Descriptive cuts are not operating segments until they alter a decision.

### Unit, Role, and State Must Stay Separate

HubSpot distinguishes individual users from target accounts, Airtable separates user intent from workspace adoption, Lovable connects product behavior with account readiness, ByteDance operates across users, products, and markets, and Duolingo separates lifecycle and payment states.

Mixing these levels creates invalid ICPs and misleading denominators.

### Identification Must Precede the Outcome

Retention and expansion can reveal a strong segment after the fact, but routing requires information available before treatment. Early intent, workflow, account, market, device, or behavior signals need validation against later value.

### Expansion Reopens Segmentation

Moving upmarket, entering a country, creating a Lite product, changing free-paid design, or adding a new AI customer type changes the conditions under which value is realized and captured.

## Transfer Matrix

| Product condition | Most relevant case | What to borrow | What not to assume |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Flexible product serves many workflows | Airtable | Ask only intent questions that change the path and validate against downstream value | That more personalization questions or collaboration always improve activation |
| B2B product is considering a market-tier move | HubSpot | Rebuild product, price, channel, sales, and economics around the chosen segment | That company size alone defines the new ICP |
| AI product has broad attention but uncertain business demand | Lovable | Combine product behavior, account readiness, retention, and monetization before routing | That hobbyists have no value or one usage threshold proves sales readiness |
| Consumer portfolio spans devices and markets | ByteDance | Use local conditions, eligibility, and portfolio checks before creating separate paths | That historical Lite-product or incentive tactics transfer directly |
| Freemium consumer product spans markets and lifecycle states | Duolingo | Segment by market, channel, intent, free-paid state, and retained behavior | That one global paywall, cadence, or learning pattern fits every segment |

## Evidence and Limits

- Company evidence comes mainly from participant interviews, talks, articles, and reconstructed company pages rather than complete internal segment dictionaries.
- The cases span different products, periods, units, markets, business models, and evidence quality.
- Identification rules, overlap, unknown rates, confusion matrices, segment cohorts, economics, experiments, and failed paths are incomplete.
- Company outcomes also reflect product value, market conditions, competition, capital, operations, leadership, and many teams beyond segmentation.
- Inclusion documents segmentation patterns and does not imply endorsement by any company or person.
