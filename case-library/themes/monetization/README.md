## Case 1: Duolingo - Freemium, Mission, and Subscription

Duolingo combines broad free access with advertising and subscription revenue. Learners pay for convenience and additional capability while the product aims to preserve a useful free learning experience.

```text
Accessible learning value
-> broad free adoption and habit
-> subscription or advertising value
-> retained learning and revenue
-> reinvestment in product and access
```

Freemium works only if free users reach real value at a sustainable cost and some path converts value into revenue. Subscription optimization must read learning quality, habit, cancellation, advertising trade-offs, and mission together.

**Primary boundary:** Duolingo's frequency, consumer category, brand, experimentation scale, and education mission make its free-paid balance unusual.

## Case 2: HubSpot - Channel-Model Fit and Tier Economics

Brian Balfour's HubSpot Sales account describes movement from an individual, low-friction freemium hypothesis toward team and mid-market economics involving tier pricing, minimum seats, annual contracts, and a sales-assisted model.

```text
Recurring sales-workflow value
-> free or low-friction adoption
-> retained product use
-> tier and minimum-seat offer
-> higher ARPU and sales support
-> CAC, retention, payback, and scale fit
```

The case shows that acquisition channel and monetization model constrain each other. Higher ARPU can fund a more expensive sales motion, while a low-ARPU touchless model requires lower CAC and strong self-service retention.

**Primary boundary:** the account covers an approximate 2014-2016 period and is not an official financial disclosure or description of current HubSpot pricing.

## Case 3: Lovable - AI Product Value and Volatile Economics

Lovable's case raises monetization questions common to AI-native creation products: users can reach value quickly, inference and tool costs vary, capabilities change rapidly, and trust affects whether customers commit.

```text
AI-assisted creation value
-> early proof and activation
-> usage, package, or subscription decision
-> model and support cost
-> retained creation value and trust
-> renew, expand, redesign, or churn
```

Earlier monetization can filter serious intent and help fund variable cost, but a paywall placed before credible proof can suppress adoption. Unlimited or annual plans simplify buying but become risky when use, capability, and unit cost remain volatile.

**Primary boundary:** fast product and model change makes historical cost, packaging, growth, and annual-plan assumptions fragile.

## Case 4: Ramp - Customer Savings and Profitable B2B Growth

Ramp frames its economic promise around helping businesses save money and operate finance more efficiently. The case connects customer outcome, business-model incentive alignment, growth measurement, contribution, payback, product velocity, and enterprise controls.

```text
Measurable customer savings and efficiency
-> trusted product adoption
-> broader finance workflow use
-> retained customer and company value
-> contribution and payback discipline
```

The promise is credible only when realized savings, pricing, revenue sources, product behavior, cost, and controls remain aligned. B2B expansion should follow additional customer value rather than sales pressure alone.

**Primary boundary:** participant accounts do not provide complete customer cohorts, contribution margins, pricing, interchange economics, or independent savings verification.

## Case 5: ByteDance - Multi-Horizon LTV and Portfolio Allocation

The ByteDance account uses predictive LTV and several ROI horizons to evaluate acquisition and portfolio decisions. Longer horizons may recognize cohorts that monetize later, while automation and product cross-promotion allocate capital and traffic at scale.

```text
Retained product and attention value
-> cohort revenue and cost observation
-> multi-horizon LTV forecast
-> market, budget, and portfolio allocation
-> back-testing, risk, and model update
```

Long-horizon economics require calibration, uncertainty ranges, cash constraints, and stop-loss rules. Portfolio movement must distinguish incremental value from cannibalization, especially when incentives or Lite products change behavior.

**Primary boundary:** figures and system descriptions come mainly from a former participant's retrospective account and cannot serve as official architecture or universal LTV benchmarks.

## Cross-Company Patterns

### Monetization Follows a Value System

- Duolingo links paid convenience to retained learning and broad access.
- HubSpot links price and sales motion to product retention and ARPU-CAC economics.
- Lovable must connect AI creation value to variable cost and trust.
- Ramp links monetization credibility to measurable customer savings.
- ByteDance connects portfolio allocation to retained attention and forecast value.

Revenue growth without retained customer value and contribution does not prove a healthy model.

### Model and Channel Must Fit

| Model condition | Required channel or operating condition |
| --- | --- |
| Low-ARPU self-service | Low CAC, short time to value, strong activation, and retention |
| Sales-assisted B2B | Sufficient ACV, proof, implementation, and payback |
| Freemium | Sustainable free cost plus activation, distribution, or upgrade contribution |
| Usage or AI consumption | Trusted metering, cost control, value correlation, and bill predictability |
| Advertising | Durable user value and explicit attention-quality guardrails |
| Portfolio allocation | Comparable value definitions, calibrated forecasts, and cannibalization checks |

### Retention and Margin Mature After Conversion

Every case requires post-purchase or post-monetization evidence: paid learning and cancellation at Duolingo; product retention and renewal at HubSpot; retained creation value and cost at Lovable; savings and workflow expansion at Ramp; matured cohort value and forecast accuracy at ByteDance.

### Complexity Has a Cost

Tiers, minimums, contracts, discounts, usage, advertising, AI credits, incentives, and portfolio rules can improve fit while increasing sales, billing, support, finance, and customer-understanding cost. Preserve only complexity that supports a stable segment or economic decision.

## Transfer Matrix

| Situation | Start with | Avoid |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer product with broad mission | Free value, frequency, cost, paid convenience, and trust | Making free intentionally ineffective |
| Self-service B2B with weak economics | Retention, ARPU, CAC, payback, and buyer path | Adding sales before deal economics support it |
| AI product with variable cost | Task value, cost by task, quality, trust, and budget control | Pricing tokens without customer-value context |
| Enterprise product with many exceptions | Stable segments, package boundaries, realized price, and migration | Letting custom contracts define the product |
| Portfolio or multi-market company | Cohort value, forecast calibration, cash limits, and cannibalization | One optimistic LTV across products and markets |
| Strong revenue, weak contribution | Serving, support, acquisition, incentive, refund, and risk cost | Scaling bookings before marginal economics mature |

## Evidence and Limits

- Evidence relies mainly on participant interviews, talks, and practitioner analysis rather than complete internal pricing, cohort, contract, cost, or experiment data.
- Company practices span different periods and do not represent every current offer or model.
- Category, mission, buyer, frequency, regulation, cost structure, sales motion, capital, and data scale limit transfer.
- Reported growth, revenue, valuation, customer, savings, or experiment-volume claims are not used as causal proof.
- Company outcomes cannot be attributed to one price, model, team, or practitioner.
