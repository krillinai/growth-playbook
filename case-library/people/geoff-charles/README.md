## Core Growth Ideas

### 1. Velocity Is Time to Validated Impact

Shipping quickly matters when work reaches users and changes an important outcome. Feature count alone can hide rework, defects, and low-value output.

### 2. Use Single-Threaded Goals

One explicit priority protects teams from diffuse requests. Ambitious goals can force different solutions, but need clear quality and customer guardrails.

### 3. Give Context, Then Autonomy

Teams move faster when they understand strategy, customer problems, constraints, and decision rights. Autonomy without context fragments the product; context without authority creates waiting.

### 4. Write to Improve Decisions

Written strategy and problem analysis expose assumptions, enable asynchronous feedback, and preserve decisions. Documents should accelerate action rather than become ceremonial planning.

### 5. Think From First Principles

Existing category conventions are inputs, not requirements. Teams should derive the user problem and constraints before copying a competitor workflow.

## Evidence and Limits

- Operating methods are Charles's participant account.
- Published feature counts do not establish customer impact or causal growth.
- High velocity can increase burnout, defects, compliance risk, and strategic churn without guardrails.
