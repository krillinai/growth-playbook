# April Dunford

> Positioning consultant, founder of Ambient Strategy, and author of *Obviously Awesome* and *Sales Pitch*. Her work turns positioning from a messaging exercise into a structured decision about competitive context, differentiated value, best-fit customers, and market category.

## Apply This Lens

Use this lens when buyers misunderstand the product, sales quality is inconsistent, positioning is reduced to messaging, or AI and portfolio claims sound generic.

1. Define the product, segment, buying situation, and positioning decision.
2. Map competitive alternatives, unique capabilities, differentiated value, best-fit customers, and the market category that makes the value obvious.
3. Test the position against customer evidence, qualification, sales narrative, portfolio coherence, and the burden of creating a category.
4. Produce one positioning thesis, contradictory evidence, validation work, and the next decisions for product, marketing, and sales.

> This lens applies frameworks documented in April Dunford's public work. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or a simulation of April Dunford.

## Growth-Related Career

Dunford's public work draws on operating and consulting experience with B2B technology companies. She focuses on products that are valuable but difficult for buyers, sales teams, or markets to understand because their competitive frame is unclear.

Positioning affects acquisition quality, sales conversion, pricing power, product strategy, launches, and retention expectations. It does not manufacture Product-Market Fit or replace product evidence.

## Core Positioning Ideas

### 1. Positioning Is Context, Not a Tagline

Positioning defines the market context that makes a product's value obvious to the right customers. Messaging, branding, campaigns, and sales stories express that context; they should not be used to discover it accidentally.

### 2. Start With Competitive Alternatives

Ask what customers would do if the product did not exist. The answer may be a direct competitor, a spreadsheet, an internal process, an agency, a general-purpose tool, or doing nothing. This reveals the real comparison shaping perceived value.

### 3. Translate Unique Capabilities Into Customer Value

List capabilities that alternatives cannot easily match, then connect each capability to an outcome customers care about. Features without consequences are weak differentiation; generic benefits such as “easy” or “powerful” rarely establish a useful position.

```text
unique capability
-> operational consequence
-> customer or business value
-> evidence
```

### 4. Define Best-Fit Customers From Value

The best target is not every customer who could use the product. Identify customers who care urgently about the differentiated value, have the conditions needed to realize it, and can recognize why the alternatives are insufficient.

### 5. Choose a Market Category That Provides Helpful Context

A category supplies assumptions about what the product is, who it is for, what alternatives exist, and how it should be evaluated. Use an existing category when it helps. Reframe or create a category only when the existing frame systematically hides the differentiated value and the company can teach the new context.

### 6. Work in Dependency Order

Dunford's positioning sequence is:

```text
competitive alternatives
-> unique attributes
-> differentiated value
-> best-fit customers
-> market category
-> trends that reinforce relevance
```

Starting with a category, persona, or slogan can lock the team into assumptions before it understands why customers choose.

### 7. Separate Positioning, Strategy, and Vision

Positioning defines the current context in which the product wins. Strategy chooses where and how the company will compete. Vision describes the future the company wants to create. They influence one another but answer different questions.

### 8. Convert Positioning Into a Sales Narrative

A sales pitch should help a buyer understand the market problem and evaluation criteria before presenting features. A common structure is:

```text
market insight
-> current alternatives and their limits
-> desired future
-> differentiated value
-> product capabilities and proof
-> objections, next step, and decision
```

### 9. Use Positioning in Qualification and Discovery

Qualification should identify whether the prospect has the problem, conditions, urgency, and priorities that make the differentiated value relevant. Discovery is not a generic questionnaire; it should test fit while helping the buyer frame the decision.

### 10. Position Product Portfolios Deliberately

Single-product and multi-product companies face different choices. A portfolio may use one umbrella position, separate product positions, or a platform-plus-products architecture. The structure should reflect buying context, shared value, cross-sell logic, and whether combining products clarifies or dilutes differentiation.

### 11. Test Positioning Through Market Behavior

Positioning is a hypothesis that needs execution and evidence. Test whether target customers understand the frame, recognize the differentiated value, progress through sales, accept pricing, adopt for the expected reason, and retain. Do not reduce testing to preference surveys about taglines.

### 12. AI Makes Context and Proof More Important

When products can copy features quickly and many vendors claim similar AI capability, the buyer needs a sharper answer to “why this product, for this situation, instead of the alternatives?” Proprietary context, workflow fit, data, trust, integration, outcomes, and proof can matter more than a generic AI label.

## Positioning System Summary

| Layer | Diagnostic question | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Alternatives | What would customers do without this product? | Win/loss, interviews, replacement behavior |
| Capabilities | What can the product do that alternatives cannot? | Product evidence, technical and operational proof |
| Value | Why do those differences matter? | Outcomes, avoided cost, speed, risk, revenue, quality |
| Best-fit customers | Who cares most and can realize the value? | Segment conversion, urgency, retention, willingness to pay |
| Category | Which context makes the value easiest to understand? | Buyer comprehension, evaluation criteria, sales progression |
| Narrative | How should the decision be explained? | Discovery quality, objection handling, pitch conversion |
| Execution | Does the position work across the journey? | Acquisition quality, sales cycle, pricing, adoption, retention |
