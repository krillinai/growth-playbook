## Growth-Related Career

Currier's public work connects company building and investing with a taxonomy of network effects. He emphasizes that growth tactics depend on the product's network structure: a labor marketplace, managed marketplace, social network, platform, protocol, data network, or market network cannot use the same launch and defense playbook.

Investment and founder accounts provide pattern recognition, not complete causal evidence. Company outcomes also depend on product value, operations, capital, regulation, competition, timing, and teams.

## Core Network-Effect Ideas

### 1. Network Effects Are Not Virality

Virality describes how a product spreads. A network effect exists when the product becomes more valuable to a participant as relevant participation, interaction, data, compatibility, or ecosystem activity grows. A product can spread without becoming more valuable, or become more valuable without viral acquisition.

### 2. Identify the Network Effect Type

Different mechanisms create value differently. Useful families include direct and personal networks, marketplaces, market networks, platforms and protocols, data and technology-performance effects, and social effects built through language, identity, belief, or status.

The taxonomy is a diagnostic map, not a checklist of badges. A claimed effect requires a causal mechanism and observable participant value.

### 3. Start With a White-Hot Center

A network should first work for a narrow group with urgent need, dense interaction, and enough relevant participants to repeatedly complete the core job. The white-hot center may be geographic, professional, categorical, social, or workflow-based.

Broad signups can hide a network that is fragmented into non-functioning clusters.

### 4. Solve the Hard Side and the Core Interaction

Identify the participant, inventory, expertise, trust, or behavior that constrains value. Acquire and retain that hard side, but measure whether the core interaction succeeds. Supply counts without availability, quality, response, matching, or repeat use do not establish liquidity.

### 5. Marketplace Type Determines the Playbook

Marketplace structure changes acquisition, operations, trust, economics, and expansion. Consider whether the market is local or global, horizontal or vertical, managed or unmanaged, B2B or consumer, high or low frequency, homogeneous or differentiated, and whether supply can multi-home.

### 6. Market Networks Combine SaaS and a Network

A market network combines professional identity, communication and workflow software, and transactions in a defined industry. The software helps participants do work; the network helps them find and coordinate with one another; the marketplace layer may enable payment or exchange.

### 7. Some Products Are Disguised Marketplaces

A product may look like SaaS, fintech, infrastructure, or a workflow tool while gradually aggregating supply, demand, services, or transactions. Identify whether product usage creates a future exchange layer and whether that layer improves customer value rather than merely extracting fees.

### 8. Network Effects Can Be Measured and Lost

Measure local density, interaction success, time to match, repeat behavior, retention by network, cross-side retention, unique supply, multi-homing, switching, concentration, contribution, and value improvement as participation grows.

Scale can weaken the network when relevance, quality, trust, economics, or participant control deteriorates.

### 9. Multi-Homing Weakens Defensibility

If participants can use several competing networks with little cost, aggregate size may not create strong defense. Evaluate unique supply, exclusive data, workflow depth, reputation portability, switching cost, transaction frequency, and whether the product becomes the system of record.

### 10. Network Effects Need Product Value Before Defense

A theoretical network effect does not compensate for weak standalone or early-network value. The product must solve a real problem at low density, reach a functioning interaction threshold, and retain participants before network reinforcement becomes a moat.

### 11. The Market Effect Reinforces Category Leaders

As a company becomes a recognized leader, customers, employees, partners, capital, press, and ecosystem attention may increasingly flow toward it. This reinforcement can support growth, but it is not a substitute for product and network value and can reverse when performance weakens.

### 12. AI Changes Participants, Not the Need for Networks

AI agents can create supply, perform work, search, negotiate, or transact. This may compress traditional marketplace roles and reduce some information advantages. Defensibility still depends on trusted relationships, proprietary interaction data, workflow position, reputation, distribution, liquidity, and governance.

### 13. Access Is an Underestimated Growth Asset

Networks can create differentiated access to customers, expertise, opportunities, capital, or distribution. Evaluate who gains access, how it improves outcomes, whether the access is scarce, and whether platform control creates dependency or unfair exclusion.

## Network-Effect System Summary

| Layer | Diagnostic question | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Core value | What useful job works before the network is large? | Activation, early retention, qualitative value |
| Network unit | Which participants must interact within which boundary? | Geography, category, role, workflow, time |
| Hard side | Which participant or asset constrains value? | Availability, quality, response, retention, economics |
| Interaction | Does the core exchange succeed reliably? | Match, completion, time, satisfaction, repeat use |
| Reinforcement | Why does added participation improve future value? | Selection, speed, data, reputation, compatibility, trust |
| Defense | Why can competitors not reproduce the value easily? | Multi-homing, switching, unique supply, workflow, data |
| Expansion | Can the network reproduce in adjacent units? | Time to density, retention, economics, operational burden |
