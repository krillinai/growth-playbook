## Why Study Her

Elena Verna is useful because her methods were developed across several companies and business stages rather than within one dominant company. Her material addresses a recurring B2B question: how should product, marketing, sales, and monetization work together as a self-serve product moves upmarket?

She also challenges tactic-first growth. Hiring a Head of Growth, running more experiments, rebranding, buying traffic, or removing every point of friction cannot compensate for an unclear growth model. Her approach starts with product value, distribution mechanics, organizational readiness, and revenue ownership.

## Core Growth Ideas

### 1. Do Not Hire Growth to Manufacture Product-Market Fit

Verna argues that companies often hire growth roles too early or expect a Head of Growth to repair foundational product problems. A growth leader can improve a working system, but cannot substitute for product value, retention, executive alignment, or basic data access.

The first growth hire should match the company's actual constraint and often comes from inside, where product context and organizational trust already exist.

### 2. Build and Evolve a Growth Model

A growth model describes how acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, and expansion reinforce one another. The model must evolve as the company changes audience, product, pricing, or route to market.

```text
Product value
-> Acquisition and activation
-> Retention and habit
-> Monetization and expansion
-> Reinvestment and distribution
```

Copying isolated tactics without identifying the company's current constraint produces activity rather than compounding growth.

### 3. Product-Led Growth Is a Company System

PLG is not simply a free plan or an acquisition channel. The product must help users discover, experience, adopt, and expand value with limited human assistance. Product, marketing, sales, data, and customer success therefore need shared ownership of the journey.

Verna argues that product-led mechanics can remain important as a company moves upmarket, but must be combined deliberately with sales rather than displaced by it.

### 4. Layer Product-Led Sales Onto Proven Product Signals

Product-Led Sales uses observed product behavior to identify accounts that may benefit from sales assistance. Verna distinguishes product-qualified accounts and users from traditional marketing-qualified leads.

Signals may include activation, breadth and depth of usage, team adoption, repeat behavior, and evidence of an enterprise-level problem. Sales should be applied where it increases customer value or resolves complexity, not simply to contact every free user.

### 5. Product Must Share Accountability for Monetization

Adding sales does not remove product's revenue responsibility. Product teams influence conversion, packaging, expansion, and the signals that make sales efficient. Verna recommends revenue-linked goals and clear ROI for the people and infrastructure added to a Product-Led Sales motion.

### 6. Freemium and Trials Solve Different Problems

Verna generally favors freemium for products that benefit from sustained product discovery, collaboration, or bottom-up adoption. A time-limited trial can end before a user reaches value or before a team naturally expands.

Freemium is not automatically correct. The free experience must deliver real value, preserve a reason to pay, and fit the product's cost structure and natural adoption cycle.

### 7. Prioritize Earned Distribution Before Paid Scale

Verna prioritizes earned channels before other forms of distribution and describes paid marketing in a company's first year as a potential "death trap." The underlying concern is that paid acquisition can hide weak positioning, trust, retention, or organic advocacy.

Earned distribution includes customer advocacy, community, social content, word of mouth, templates, ecosystems, and product outputs people naturally share. Paid channels become more useful after the company understands conversion and retention economics.

### 8. Growth Is Increasingly a Trust Problem

For AI products, capability alone does not guarantee adoption. Users must trust the product, the company, and the claims around what it can produce. Founder visibility, employee participation, credible demonstrations, and customer-created proof can reduce that trust gap.

At Lovable, Verna describes employees across functions acting as marketers and even shipping code. The reusable idea is tight contact between product creation and public communication, not copying a specific social-posting cadence.

### 9. Experimentation Is a Tool, Not the Strategy

More experiments do not necessarily produce more growth. Teams can over-experiment on low-impact surfaces while avoiding product, positioning, or model changes. Button colors, isolated emails, and friction removal may improve local conversion without changing durable value.

Friction should be evaluated by purpose. Some friction qualifies intent, improves setup, captures information needed for service, or protects trust. The question is whether a step creates more downstream value than cost.

### 10. Rebrands and Competitor Obsession Rarely Create Durable Growth

A rebrand can clarify an existing strategy but cannot create demand by itself. Similarly, competitor monitoring becomes harmful when it replaces customer research and understanding of the company's own growth model.

Verna's recurring recommendation is to diagnose the internal constraint before reaching for a visible external tactic.

### 11. Product Launches Need Concentrated, Cross-Functional Distribution

At Lovable, launches operate as coordinated product and distribution events, not marketing announcements added after development. Product changes, demonstrations, founder and employee communication, customer proof, and social response reinforce one another.

This approach depends on a product worth demonstrating and an audience that trusts the messengers. Launch volume cannot replace substance.

## Growth System Summary

| Layer | Verna's approach | Operating implication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Foundation | Product value and retention before growth hiring | Do not outsource PMF to a growth leader |
| Model | Connect acquisition, retention, monetization, and expansion | Diagnose the system constraint before selecting tactics |
| PLG | Let users discover and experience value through the product | Treat PLG as cross-functional infrastructure |
| PLS | Use product signals to identify accounts needing assistance | Apply sales selectively and measure incremental value |
| Monetization | Keep product accountable for revenue outcomes | Connect roadmap and packaging decisions to business results |
| Distribution | Build earned channels before paid scale | Invest in advocacy, community, content, and product sharing |
| Experimentation | Test important hypotheses, not everything measurable | Prioritize model and product changes over cosmetic volume |
| AI growth | Build trust through credible product proof | Connect builders, users, and public communication |
| Organization | Hire for the current constraint and stage | Match role, authority, data, and expectations |

## How to Apply the Method

1. Map the current growth model across acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, and expansion.
2. Identify the largest system constraint before selecting a tactic or hiring a leader.
3. Define what users can discover and accomplish without human assistance.
4. Add sales only where product signals indicate complexity, value, or expansion potential.
5. Give product teams measurable responsibility for monetization outcomes.
6. Design free access around time to value, natural usage frequency, cost, and a reason to upgrade.
7. Establish earned distribution before using paid acquisition to scale.
8. Prioritize experiments by expected strategic impact, not ease of measurement.
9. Review the growth model whenever audience, product, pricing, or route to market changes.
10. For AI products, measure trust and credible proof alongside activation and revenue.

## Limitations and Caveats

- Current and previous roles can change over time; this profile reflects her career through March 2026.
- The methods span 2022 to 2026 and several companies, market conditions, and stages. They should not be combined without context.
- This profile does not use Lovable's reported growth rate or revenue as evidence for the effectiveness of a specific method.
- "Tactics that never work" is intentionally provocative framing. Some tactics can work when they address a diagnosed constraint and are measured against downstream value.
- Freemium, PLG, PLS, and earned-first distribution depend on product economics, buying process, implementation complexity, regulation, and customer segment.
- Advisory experience provides broad pattern recognition but can also compress important differences between companies.
- Inclusion of a person's methods does not endorse every company decision, management practice, or public position.
