## Growth-Related Work

Isford describes Airtable's onboarding work as a progression from generic interface guidance toward helping different users build a meaningful workflow. The team combined qualitative understanding, segmentation, experiments, and downstream collaboration measures.

Her approach separates completing onboarding from reaching value. A user can dismiss tooltips or finish a wizard without adopting the product; activation must therefore connect early actions to later retained behavior.

## Core Growth Ideas

### 1. Define Activation From Customer Outcomes

Activation should represent progress toward a real outcome, not completion of an arbitrary setup checklist. The definition must be tested against later retention and valuable use.

### 2. Segment the Learning Experience

Users arrive with different goals and levels of product familiarity. Guided setup can ask enough to provide a relevant path without turning personalization into a long questionnaire.

### 3. Reduce Dependence on Tooltips

Interface annotations explain controls but rarely communicate why a workflow matters. Examples, templates, guided building, and contextual education can bring the user closer to an actual result.

### 4. Measure Collaboration Downstream

For a collaborative product, value may appear when more than one person uses a workspace. Isford discusses a week-four multi-user collaboration measure as a stronger signal than an isolated first-session action.

### 5. Use Experiments With Guardrails

An onboarding change can raise a local conversion metric while reducing quality, flexibility, or later retention. Experiments need a primary outcome and guardrails that capture downstream costs.

## Evidence and Limits

- Isford reports that an onboarding redesign increased activation by 20%; the interview does not provide the full experiment design, metric definition, confidence interval, or independent replication.
- The account reflects Airtable's product, users, and measurement system during the period discussed.
- Collaboration is a plausible value signal for multi-user software, but it is not the right North Star for every product or segment.
