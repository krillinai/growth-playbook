## Core Growth Ideas

### 1. Retention Is the Main PMF Evidence

Acquisition can create an initial spike without proving durable value. Winters treats cohort retention as the stronger signal: do users continue receiving enough value to return at the product's natural frequency?

Retention benchmarks must match category, cadence, and user intent. A flattening curve can indicate a retained segment, but the team still needs to understand who remains, why they remain, and whether the behavior supports a viable business.

### 2. Onboarding Should Create Setup, Aha, and Habit

Winters separates onboarding into three jobs:

```text
Setup -> Aha -> Habit
```

- **Setup:** collect the minimum information or connections required to make value possible.
- **Aha:** help the user experience the core value, not merely finish registration.
- **Habit:** create a credible path to repeated value at the product's natural cadence.

Teams should optimize downstream retention, not onboarding completion alone. Removing every step can increase conversion while leaving users unprepared to receive value.

### 3. Growth Models Matter More Than Framework Collection

Winters warns against using popular frameworks as substitutes for understanding a business. A useful growth model describes how acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, and loops interact for the specific product.

The model should expose the current constraint and show which input can change the outcome. Framework vocabulary without causal structure creates activity without diagnosis.

### 4. Marketplace Growth Starts With the Product Called Supply

In marketplaces, the customer experience depends on the availability, quality, relevance, and economics of supply. Winters summarizes this by saying the product is often the supply, not the software alone.

Marketplace teams need to decide whether supply should be comprehensive, exclusive, or curated, and whether demand or supply is the current constraint. Aggregate participants do not prove local or categorical liquidity.

### 5. Marketplace Expansion Requires a Repeatable Unit

A marketplace should expand only after it can reproduce value in an adjacent geography or category. Expansion changes density, operations, participant economics, and competitive behavior.

Useful questions include:

1. Is the existing market retained and economically healthy?
2. Does the new category share demand, supply, or operational capabilities?
3. Can the team reach liquidity without permanent subsidy?
4. Will expansion strengthen the core or fragment attention and inventory?

### 6. Network Effects Can Be Lost

Network effects are not permanent. Winters uses Grubhub, DoorDash, and Uber Eats to illustrate how a competitor can change the network structure, acquire a harder side, improve logistics, or bundle adjacent value.

A network moat must be measured through participant behavior: unique supply, multi-homing, liquidity, repeat transactions, service quality, and switching cost. Historical scale alone does not defend the network.

### 7. SEO Is a Product and Supply System

The Pinterest material treats SEO as more than keyword writing. Scalable landing pages can be created from structured supply, user-generated content, or deliberately produced content, but each page must satisfy a real query and deliver useful value.

```text
Structured supply or content
-> indexable page for a specific intent
-> useful logged-out experience
-> qualified conversion path
-> activated and retained user
```

Traffic without page quality, intent match, activation, and retention is not sustainable acquisition.

### 8. Conversion Optimization Begins at the Actual Entry Page

Pinterest users often entered through pins, boards, and topic pages rather than the homepage. Optimizing only the homepage therefore ignored much of the reachable audience.

Winters' examples combine quantitative experiments with qualitative research. Conversion gains should be balanced with comprehension, user happiness, and downstream retention. A signup wall can increase registrations while weakening trust or product understanding.

### 9. Sustainable Acquisition Moves From Kindle to Fire

Early companies often use manual, unscalable “kindle” strategies to find a retained customer segment. Once the mechanism is understood, “fire” strategies create repeatable and scalable acquisition.

Scaling before PMF magnifies leakage. Staying manual forever prevents compounding. The transition requires a clear customer segment, retained value, channel-model fit, and evidence that marginal economics survive volume.

### 10. Growth Teams Evolve With the Constraint

Winters describes growth organizations changing from generalist, centralized teams toward specialized ownership as companies mature. Data capability and decision rights matter as much as the org chart.

Growth teams should own meaningful outcomes and the levers required to move them. A team that only recommends work to other teams lacks execution authority; a team that owns every surface can become a maintenance organization.

### 11. Product Leadership Requires Selling the Reasoning

Product leaders need to explain why an unglamorous improvement matters, reduce decision risk before meetings, and make complex reasoning appear simple without hiding assumptions.

Influence is not presentation polish alone. It requires customer evidence, business context, tradeoffs, anticipated objections, and a decision that the audience can act on.

### 12. AI Knowledge Products Need Trust and Consent

The SuperMe discussion focuses on representing professional judgment from writing, conversations, and other artifacts. The product thesis depends on sourcing, attribution, approval, redaction, feedback, and consent by default.

Making expertise searchable can create network and data advantages, but only if people trust how their perspective is represented. Content volume or virality is not a substitute for faithful, permissioned knowledge.

## Operating Summary

| Layer | Winters' approach | Diagnostic question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| PMF | Read retention by cohort and segment | Who continues receiving value, and why? |
| Onboarding | Setup, Aha, Habit | Does the first experience prepare repeated value? |
| Model | Map mechanisms and constraints | Which input currently limits growth? |
| Marketplace | Treat supply as part of the product | Is relevant supply available and economically healthy? |
| Expansion | Repeat a functioning market unit | Will the next market strengthen or fragment the system? |
| SEO | Turn useful structured content into entry pages | Does search traffic reach value and retain? |
| Conversion | Optimize actual entry surfaces | Does higher conversion preserve comprehension and quality? |
| Acquisition | Move from kindle to fire | Is the mechanism ready to scale economically? |
| Organization | Match ownership to the constraint | Can the team execute on the outcome it owns? |
| AI trust | Preserve consent and attribution | Is represented expertise faithful and controllable? |
