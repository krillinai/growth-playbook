## Company Case: Ramp: Growth and Measurement

### Case at a Glance

| Field | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Company | Ramp |
| Growth problem | Scale B2B growth while preserving economic and learning quality |
| Main mechanisms | Growth engineering, acquisition and expansion metrics, payback, contribution margin, experiment portfolios, postmortems |
| Participant perspectives | Sri Batchu and George Bonaci |

### Case Summary

Ramp's described growth system separates growth volume from growth quality. New customers and account expansion are measured distinctly; payback and contribution margin constrain investment; growth engineering connects product and GTM systems; experiments are judged by whether they produce a decision.

```text
Customer value
-> Acquisition or expansion hypothesis
-> Economic and quality constraints
-> Product, data, and GTM execution
-> Conclusive evidence
-> Scale, revise, or stop
```

### Operating Principles

#### Separate Growth Motions

New-logo acquisition, activation, usage expansion, cross-sell, and retention have different denominators and time horizons. Reporting them separately prevents one strong motion from hiding another weak one.

#### Measure Volume and Efficiency Together

Revenue or customer growth without payback and contribution margin can consume cash without creating durable value. Efficiency metrics also need cohort maturity, shared-cost rules, and sensitivity analysis.

#### Give Growth Engineering a Clear Customer

Growth engineering can build product surfaces, data pipelines, sales tools, and automation. Its roadmap should connect to a defined bottleneck and downstream outcome rather than becoming a general request queue.

#### Fail Conclusively

An experiment can win, lose, or reveal no meaningful difference. The first two may produce learning; an underpowered or poorly instrumented test often does not. Teams should predefine the hypothesis, decision threshold, guardrails, and next action.

#### Use Portfolios and Postmortems

A mix of incremental and exploratory bets balances reliability and discovery. Postmortems turn individual results into changes to targeting, instrumentation, process, or strategy.

### Transferable Method

1. Split acquisition, activation, expansion, retention, and cross-sell cohorts.
2. Pair each volume metric with quality and economic guardrails.
3. Calculate payback from contribution margin, not gross revenue alone.
4. Allocate capacity across core optimization, adjacencies, and exploration.
5. Write decision criteria before launching an experiment.
6. Record what changed after each meaningful result.

### Evidence and Limits

- The case synthesizes participant accounts from 2023 and 2025.
- Complete internal definitions, cohorts, experiment logs, and economics are unavailable.
- Publisher hypergrowth claims are not used as causal validation.

## Company Case: Ramp: Product Velocity

### Case at a Glance

| Field | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Company | Ramp |
| Growth problem | Build and expand a trusted financial product faster than established competitors |
| Main mechanisms | Customer savings, B2B UX, single-threaded goals, autonomy, writing, first principles, technical builders, hiring |
| Participant perspectives | Geoff Charles, Karim Atiyeh, and Eric Glyman |

### Case Summary

Ramp's product-velocity model starts with a differentiated economic promise: help customers save money and operating time. Teams receive context and focused goals, use writing to expose assumptions, and build quickly across conventional functional boundaries.

```text
Distinct customer outcome
-> Focused goal and constraints
-> Context and decision rights
-> Fast build and customer feedback
-> Quality, savings, and control checks
-> Expand or revise
```

### Operating Principles

#### Make Speed Serve Differentiation

Shipping faster matters when it strengthens a distinct customer outcome. A broad finance platform should expand from proven problems rather than accumulate unrelated features.

#### Combine Consumer UX With Enterprise Controls

Clarity and low friction can differentiate B2B software, but finance workflows also require permissions, audit trails, security, reliability, and policy enforcement.

#### Focus Teams on One Important Outcome

Single-threaded goals reduce coordination cost and protect teams from diffuse requests. Leaders provide strategy, customer context, constraints, and decision rights rather than prescribing every task.

#### Use Writing and First Principles

Written thinking makes tradeoffs inspectable and supports asynchronous decisions. First-principles analysis helps teams question category conventions while preserving real regulatory and customer constraints.

#### Hire Complementary Spikes

Exceptional strengths can raise a team's ceiling when roles and collaboration fit. Talent density is not permission for harmful behavior, weak documentation, or unmanaged key-person risk.

### Transferable Method

1. Define the customer outcome that makes the product economically different.
2. Give each team one primary outcome and explicit guardrails.
3. Share customer, strategic, technical, and regulatory context.
4. Write assumptions before major product decisions.
5. Measure cycle time through customer validation, not release count.
6. Track defects, trust, savings, adoption, and team sustainability.

### Evidence and Limits

- The case synthesizes executive and product-leader accounts.
- Feature, revenue, valuation, and growth claims do not prove that velocity caused outcomes.
- Financial-product controls and Ramp's talent market limit direct transfer.

## Company Case: Ramp: AI Marketing Organization

### Case at a Glance

| Field | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Company | Ramp |
| Period discussed | 2026 |
| Growth problem | Increase marketing leverage without preserving functional work that AI can automate |
| Main mechanisms | Shared agents, internal context, workflow redesign, human review, brand, events, mixed-method measurement |
| Participant perspective | George Bonaci, VP of Growth |

### Case Summary

Ramp's described approach does not add isolated AI tools to every marketer. It reorganizes repeated research, content, campaign, and operational work around shared agents and an internal system called Project Glass. People retain responsibility for goals, context, creative judgment, validation, and coordination.

```text
Business objective and trusted context
-> Agent-assisted research or production
-> Human judgment and validation
-> Distribution across product, brand, and events
-> Experimental and business measurement
-> Updated context and workflow
```

### Operating Principles

#### Redesign the Workflow, Not Only the Task

Automating one step can move the bottleneck elsewhere. Teams should map inputs, decisions, handoffs, review, distribution, and learning before deciding where an agent belongs.

#### Treat Context as Infrastructure

Agents need current product facts, audience definitions, brand rules, examples, performance data, and permissions. Retrieval quality, provenance, access control, and update ownership determine reliability.

#### Keep Humans Accountable

People define the problem, inspect claims, handle ambiguity, approve brand risk, and own outcomes. AI output volume is not a marketing result.

#### Measure Attention Beyond Last Touch

Brand, physical items, and in-person events can influence demand without producing a clean last-click path. Measurement can combine holdouts, geo or audience tests, direct traffic, branded search, customer interviews, pipeline quality, and long-term cohorts.

#### Preserve Distinctive Brand Judgment

AI makes average content cheaper. Advantage shifts toward proprietary context, useful insight, creative taste, trusted distribution, and experiences competitors cannot generate from the same prompt.

### Transferable Method

1. Map a complete marketing workflow and its bottleneck.
2. Define trusted context, permissions, owners, and freshness rules.
3. Automate bounded steps with test datasets and human review.
4. Measure accuracy, cycle time, cost, rework, brand quality, and downstream outcomes.
5. Keep a manual fallback and logs for material decisions.
6. Use experiments and customer evidence for channels that resist last-touch attribution.

### Evidence and Limits

- Current evidence is centered on one June 2026 participant interview.
- Project Glass architecture, model choices, costs, accuracy, adoption, and business lift are not fully disclosed.
- Organizational changes may evolve rapidly as models and Ramp's strategy change.
