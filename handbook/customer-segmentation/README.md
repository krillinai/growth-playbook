# Customer Segmentation & ICP

> Segmentation divides a market or customer base into groups that require different decisions. An Ideal Customer Profile identifies the customers most likely to realize, retain, and support the intended value.

**[English](README.md)｜[简体中文](README.zh.md)**

## Definitions and Boundaries

| Concept | Purpose | Boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Market segment | Groups customers with meaningfully different needs, conditions, behavior, value, or economics | A segment must change a product, positioning, channel, sales, service, or monetization decision |
| ICP | Describes the customer conditions under which the product can deliver and capture strong value | It is an evidence-backed operating hypothesis, not a list of aspirational logos |
| Persona | Represents a role, motivation, workflow, or decision context within a segment or account | A persona does not establish market attractiveness or account fit |
| Cohort | Groups users by start time, exposure, behavior, or product state for measurement | Cohorts diagnose performance; they are not automatically durable market segments |

Demographics and firmographics can help locate customers, but they rarely explain fit alone. Useful segmentation connects observable differences to different customer jobs, value paths, constraints, willingness to pay, buying processes, retention, or economics.

## Segmentation Dimensions

| Dimension | Questions | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Problem and job | What outcome is the customer trying to achieve? How urgent is it? | Interviews, replacement behavior, workflow observation, search and sales language |
| Existing alternative | What happens if the product does not exist? | Win/loss, switching, spreadsheets, internal processes, services, direct competitors, doing nothing |
| Ability to realize value | Does the customer have the data, authority, collaborators, integrations, frequency, or operating maturity required? | Activation depth, implementation, time to value, usage prerequisites, support needs |
| Behavior | Which actions, frequency, breadth, or depth predict repeated value? | Product events, cohorts, retention, expansion, collaboration, feature adoption |
| Value and economics | How much value is created and captured? | Willingness to pay, conversion, retention, margin, service cost, LTV, payback |
| Buying process | Who discovers, evaluates, approves, uses, and renews? | Sales calls, procurement, security review, role mapping, cycle length |
| Route to market | Which channel and motion can reach and serve the segment sustainably? | Channel conversion, sales efficiency, retained CAC, partner fit, geography |
| Risk and constraints | What regulatory, trust, technical, organizational, or switching conditions change adoption? | Security requirements, compliance, migration cost, data sensitivity, dependency |

Do not split a segment merely because a dashboard can. Split when the distinction can change a decision and has enough evidence and volume to evaluate.

## ICP Evidence System

An ICP should answer five questions:

1. **Need:** Which important problem or job exists?
2. **Fit:** Under which conditions can the customer receive the differentiated value?
3. **Behavior:** Which observable actions show that value is being realized repeatedly?
4. **Economics:** Can the company acquire, serve, retain, and expand the customer sustainably?
5. **Access:** Can the company reliably identify and reach similar customers?

Use an evidence ledger rather than one composite score:

| Claim | Supporting evidence | Contradictory evidence | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Segment experiences urgent problem | Repeated customer language, replacement behavior, short time to first action | Curiosity without implementation | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Product creates differentiated value | Faster or better outcome, retained use, willingness to switch | Generic feature interest, heavy manual rescue | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Segment retains and expands | Mature cohorts, repeat usage, renewal, breadth or depth growth | Concentrated usage, incentive dependence, high churn | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Route to market is sustainable | Retained conversion, sales efficiency, payback, reachable audience | High service cost, channel saturation, long unsupported cycle | Verified, inferred, or missing |

The ICP is stronger when qualitative explanations and behavioral, retention, revenue, and cost evidence agree. A famous customer, a high contract value, or enthusiastic interview does not establish repeatable fit by itself.

## Segmentation Workflow

1. Define the decision: product path, positioning, acquisition, sales coverage, pricing, service, or expansion.
2. Start from customer jobs, alternatives, and conditions required to receive value.
3. Form a small number of segment hypotheses using observable criteria.
4. Compare activation, time to value, retention, monetization, service cost, and acquisition quality by segment.
5. Interview supporting, contradictory, churned, and non-converting customers.
6. Select the best-fit segment and explicitly record excluded or secondary segments.
7. Change the experience or operating decision only when the segment difference is material.
8. Revalidate when product, market, price, channel, or customer mix changes.

## When to Create Different Paths

Different product or go-to-market paths are justified when segments require materially different:

- jobs, setup, data, collaborators, or time to value;
- positioning, proof, category, or objections;
- channels, sales assistance, procurement, or service;
- packaging, price metric, willingness to pay, or support level;
- security, compliance, localization, or integration requirements.

Do not add onboarding questions, plans, sales handoffs, or content variants unless the answers change what happens next. Segmentation that adds friction without improving downstream value is taxonomy work, not growth.

## Metric System

| Layer | Metrics |
| --- | --- |
| Reach | Addressable accounts or users, identifiable share, qualified traffic, target-account coverage |
| Value entry | Qualified activation, time to value, setup success, implementation completion |
| Repeated value | Cohort retention, meaningful frequency, breadth, depth, collaboration, renewal |
| Commercial fit | Conversion, willingness to pay, ARPA or ACV, expansion, gross margin, service cost |
| Distribution fit | Retained CAC, payback, sales cycle, channel concentration, partner contribution |
| Guardrails | Support burden, complaints, exclusion harm, privacy, fairness, compliance, concentration risk |

Report rates and absolute volume. A small segment can show excellent rates without supporting the intended business scale; a large segment can create revenue while hiding weak value and high service cost.

## Common Mistakes

- Defining the ICP from company size and industry alone.
- Treating the current customer list as proof of the best future segment.
- Calling every analytical cohort a market segment.
- Using personas as substitutes for behavioral and economic evidence.
- Choosing the largest market instead of the customers who value the differentiation most.
- Contacting every active free user rather than using product and account signals.
- Creating many paths before the sample, operations, and evidence can support them.
- Ignoring customers who churned, failed implementation, or required exceptional manual work.

## Evidence and Limits

- Segment performance depends on definitions, maturity windows, sample size, selection, and changing customer mix.
- Historical fit does not guarantee future fit after product, channel, price, market, or competitive change.
- Sensitive attributes require privacy, fairness, legal, and ethical review; use only what is necessary for a legitimate decision.
- Segmentation is a decision model, not a claim that customers within a group are identical.
