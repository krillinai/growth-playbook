# Positioning

> Positioning defines the competitive context that makes a product's differentiated value clear to its best-fit customers.

**[English](README.md)｜[简体中文](README.zh.md)**

## Positioning and Growth

Positioning sits between Product-Market Fit and growth execution. It determines which customers, alternatives, value, category, messages, channels, sales motions, and pricing assumptions should be tested.

```text
Product and customer evidence
-> positioning
-> messaging and sales narrative
-> acquisition and qualification
-> adoption, monetization, and retention evidence
-> positioning revision
```

Positioning cannot repair weak product value. It can expose that weakness by making the expected customer, use case, and value explicit.

## The Five Components

### Competitive Alternatives

Identify what customers would use or do without the product. Include direct competitors, internal tools, manual work, services, general-purpose software, and inaction.

### Unique Capabilities

List capabilities the alternatives cannot match under the relevant conditions. Require product, technical, operational, data, workflow, ecosystem, trust, or service evidence.

### Differentiated Value

Translate capabilities into consequences customers care about:

```text
capability -> consequence -> customer value -> proof
```

Value may involve revenue, cost, time, risk, quality, access, control, or strategic flexibility. Avoid benefits that every competitor can claim.

### Best-Fit Customers

Define customers who care strongly about the differentiated value and have the conditions required to realize it. Use urgency, use case, existing alternatives, workflow, company state, constraints, buying process, willingness to pay, adoption, and retention.

### Market Category

Choose the context that gives buyers useful assumptions about what the product is, how it should be evaluated, and which alternatives matter. Category creation carries a substantial education cost and should not be the default.

## Positioning Sequence

Work in dependency order:

1. Competitive alternatives
2. Unique capabilities
3. Differentiated value
4. Best-fit customers
5. Market category
6. Relevant trends

Use trends only to explain urgency or relevance after the differentiated value is clear. A trend is not the position itself.

## Positioning Evidence Ledger

| Claim | Useful evidence | Common failure |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Customers compare us with X | Win/loss, sales calls, replacement behavior | Using an internal competitor list |
| Capability Y is unique | Product and operational proof | Treating roadmap claims as current facts |
| Difference Y creates value Z | Outcome data and customer language | Stopping at a feature or generic benefit |
| Segment A is best fit | Conversion, urgency, retention, willingness to pay | Defining the largest possible market |
| Category C clarifies value | Comprehension and evaluation behavior | Choosing the most fashionable category |

Label every material claim `verified`, `inferred`, or `missing`.

## Positioning Workshop

Include product, sales, marketing, customer success, and leadership participants who hold different evidence. Set the scope before the session: product, segment, geography, route to market, and decision horizon.

The workshop should produce decisions and unresolved evidence gaps, not a polished slogan. Assign owners to validate disputed alternatives, capabilities, value claims, segments, and category choices.

## Sales Narrative

Translate the position into a decision story:

1. Market insight or change
2. Existing alternatives and their limits
3. Desired future state
4. Differentiated value
5. Product capabilities and proof
6. Objections, decision criteria, and next step

The narrative should help buyers understand why the evaluation criteria matter. It should not hide weak evidence behind storytelling.

## Qualification and Discovery

Build qualification from the position. Test whether the prospect:

- has the relevant problem and current alternative;
- experiences the constraints the product solves;
- values the differentiated outcomes;
- has the workflow, authority, urgency, budget, and readiness required;
- can realize value without unacceptable implementation risk.

Measure qualified progression, win/loss reasons, sales cycle, price acceptance, implementation success, activation, and retention.

## Single-Product and Portfolio Positioning

Use one product position when the buying context is coherent. For portfolios, choose among:

- independent product positions;
- an umbrella company position with product-specific value;
- a platform position with modules or products;
- segment-specific positions built from shared capabilities.

Avoid combining products when the umbrella becomes generic or forces buyers to understand capabilities they do not need.

## Testing and Execution

Test positioning through behavior across the journey:

| Stage | Signals |
| --- | --- |
| Awareness | Correct audience and problem recognition |
| Acquisition | Qualified response and channel-message fit |
| Sales | Discovery quality, objections, progression, win/loss |
| Monetization | Price acceptance, packaging fit, discount pressure |
| Adoption | Expected use case and time to value |
| Retention | Durable value for the intended segment |

Run message tests as supporting evidence, not as a substitute for market behavior.

## AI Positioning

“AI-powered” is rarely sufficient differentiation. Evaluate proprietary data, workflow integration, output quality, reliability, control, security, compliance, switching cost, ecosystem, service, and verified outcomes. Define which alternative the AI product replaces and why the target customer should trust it.

## Metrics

- best-fit segment share of qualified pipeline;
- qualified-to-opportunity and opportunity-to-win conversion;
- sales cycle and discount rate by segment;
- win/loss reasons and alternative displaced;
- value-message comprehension;
- activation and retention by promised use case;
- expansion and willingness to pay;
- positioning-related objection frequency.

## Guardrails

- Do not manufacture unsupported differentiation.
- Do not redefine the category to avoid real competitors.
- Do not confuse a market trend with customer value.
- Do not use positioning to target customers the product cannot serve.
- Do not claim causal impact without experiments or credible comparison.
