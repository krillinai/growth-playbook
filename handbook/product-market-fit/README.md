# Product-Market Fit

> Product-Market Fit is a defensible judgment that a defined market repeatedly receives meaningful value from a product. It is not proof of distribution, viable economics, scale, one score, or a permanent badge.

**[English](README.md)｜[简体中文](README.zh.md)**

## Definition and Boundaries

PMF connects a specific market, problem, product, and repeated outcome. It is stronger than interest, signup growth, satisfaction, revenue, or a single survey score.

| Term | Meaning | Boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Problem evidence | A defined group experiences an important job, pain, or desired outcome | Frequency and severity do not prove the product solves it. |
| Product value | The product helps the group make meaningful progress | A successful first experience does not prove repeated value. |
| Product-Market Fit | A defined segment repeatedly receives enough value to continue using, adopting, or paying | Fit can remain narrow, economically weak, or difficult to distribute. |
| Growth | More retained customer and business value is created over time | Acquisition can amplify fit but cannot manufacture it. |
| Scale | The mechanism works across more volume, segments, markets, or products | Scale can weaken the fit that existed in an initial niche. |

PMF is a diagnosis about a defined unit. State the customer or participant, use case, geography, product, payer, natural frequency, and time period before evaluating it.

## Fit System

    Defined market and problem
    -> product promise
    -> first value
    -> repeated value and retention
    -> willingness to adopt or pay
    -> repeatable distribution and viable economics

PMF covers the repeated market-product value relationship inside this broader system. Distribution and economics determine whether that fit can support the intended growth and business model.

The chain is only as strong as its weakest material constraint. A product may have retained users but no viable price, strong demand but no repeat use, or healthy economics in a segment too small to support the intended business.

## Unit of Fit

PMF should be evaluated at the smallest unit that preserves a coherent problem and value mechanism.

| Product topology | Useful unit of fit | Evidence that aggregates can hide |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Consumer product | User x use case x natural frequency | A highly retained core hidden inside broad low-intent acquisition |
| B2B product | Role x workflow x account type x payer | Strong end-user use with weak buyer value or account retention |
| Collaborative product | Individual value x workspace or account adoption | Invitations or seats without repeated collaborative work |
| Marketplace | Participant side x atomic market x category | Global growth with weak local liquidity or one unhealthy side |
| Multi-product company | Product x segment x portfolio relationship | Cross-promotion or revenue that cannibalizes retained value elsewhere |
| AI product | Task x result quality x user or account context | Trial volume or prompt activity without reliable repeated outcomes |

Fit in one unit should not be generalized to adjacent segments without fresh evidence.

## Evidence Stack

No single signal proves PMF. Stronger judgments reconcile several evidence layers and keep contradictions visible.

| Layer | Evidence | Diagnostic question | Common false positive |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Problem | Frequency, severity, alternatives, workarounds, switching behavior | Is the problem important enough to change behavior? | Interview enthusiasm without costly current behavior |
| First value | Successful value event, time to value, qualitative explanation | Can qualified users reach a meaningful outcome? | Onboarding or interface completion |
| Repeated value | Mature retention by value-bearing behavior and natural interval | Does value recur without constant reacquisition? | Aggregate activity driven by new cohorts |
| Declared dependence | Must-have survey and reasons from a qualified user group | Would successful users materially miss the product? | High score from fans, employees, or premature respondents |
| Segment | Retention, value, and dependence by use case or customer condition | Where is fit strongest, weakest, and different? | One blended score across incompatible users |
| Organic pull | Direct demand, return behavior, referrals, customer language, sales pull | Does value create voluntary demand or advocacy? | Public attention that does not activate or retain |
| Economics | Willingness to pay, contribution, payback, cost to serve | Can delivered value support a viable model? | Revenue before variable cost, churn, or channel cost |
| Distribution | Incremental qualified acquisition and channel-model fit | Can the market be reached repeatedly without destroying economics? | A temporary channel spike or subsidized growth |

Confidence should increase when independent signals agree and decrease when evidence is immature, selected, modeled, or contradictory.

## Sean Ellis Test

The Sean Ellis Test asks users how they would feel if they could no longer use the product. The often-cited 40% “very disappointed” result is a heuristic, not a universal certification.

Use a qualified sample:

- users have experienced the core value;
- enough time has passed to judge repeated usefulness;
- respondents belong to a named segment or use case;
- acquisition source, tenure, plan, role, and activity are recorded;
- open-text reasons are analyzed alongside the score;
- non-response and survivorship bias remain visible.

    Very disappointed rate
    = qualified respondents choosing "very disappointed"
      / all qualified respondents who answered

Report sample size, eligibility, response rate, confidence interval, field dates, and segment distribution. Compare the answers with observed activation, retention, expansion, and churn rather than converting the score into a pass/fail gate.

## Retention Evidence

Retention is the strongest widely applicable behavioral evidence that value repeats, but it must use the right behavior, cohort, and interval.

    Qualified cohort
    -> reaches first value
    -> repeats the value-bearing behavior
    -> retains over the natural product cycle

Useful analysis includes:

- acquisition, activation, and calendar cohorts;
- user, account, revenue, supply, and demand retention where relevant;
- behavior-based retention rather than login alone;
- frequency distributions for products with heterogeneous cadence;
- resurrection and expansion separated from original retention;
- mature cohorts compared at the same age;
- segment-level curves with absolute cohort sizes.

A flattening curve supports the existence of a retained group. It does not establish market size, causal mechanism, margin, or the ability to acquire more similar users.

## Qualitative Evidence

Interviews explain the mechanism behind behavior. Prioritize users who repeatedly receive value, users who tried and left, buyers who did not adopt, and customers whose behavior changed.

| Evidence | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Trigger | What changed immediately before the search for a solution? |
| Alternative | What did the customer use, tolerate, or pay for before? |
| Progress | What outcome became easier, faster, safer, or more valuable? |
| Tradeoff | What cost, risk, setup, behavior, or dependency did adoption require? |
| Repetition | Why does the problem or desired outcome return? |
| Loss | What would happen if the product disappeared? |
| Language | Which words does the customer use to describe the problem and value? |

Do not treat compliments, feature requests, or willingness to join a waitlist as equivalent to changed behavior.

## Four Connected Fits

| Fit | Question | Evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Market-Product Fit | Does the product solve an important problem for a defined market? | Value event, retention, customer explanation, alternatives |
| Product-Channel Fit | Does the product fit how a channel distributes and converts demand? | Channel mechanics, activation quality, marginal reach |
| Channel-Model Fit | Can channel economics support the business model? | CAC, payback, contribution, sales or conversion cost |
| Model-Market Fit | Can market size, price, and cost support the intended business? | Reachable customers, ARPU, gross margin, attainable share |

These fits constrain one another. A change to market, product, channel, price, packaging, or sales motion should trigger review of the other hypotheses.

## Model-Market Fit

Model-Market Fit tests whether the target market and revenue model can support the intended scale.

    annual revenue per customer
    x reachable customers in the defined market
    x realistically attainable share
    = potential annual revenue

The equation is a hypothesis, not a forecast. Market definition, willingness to pay, reachable customer count, competitive response, churn, gross margin, and time to capture share can invalidate it.

A narrow initial segment can be correct for finding retained value while remaining too small for the long-term revenue target. Document possible expansion markets and which Fits must be reproven at each transition.

## Economics and Willingness to Pay

Free use, revenue, and PMF are different signals. Economic evidence asks whether the value can support the customer relationship and the delivery system.

Check:

- payer, beneficiary, and decision maker;
- price metric and willingness to pay;
- gross and contribution margin;
- implementation, support, inference, fulfillment, or supply cost;
- acquisition cost and payback;
- retention and expansion by plan or segment;
- discount and incentive dependency;
- cash, capacity, trust, and regulatory constraints.

Strong retention with structurally negative contribution may prove user value without proving a viable business. Strong early revenue may reflect contracts, novelty, or switching cost without proving repeated customer value.

## Segment and Expansion

PMF is usually found in a narrow set of conditions before it is broadened.

    Find a retained segment
    -> describe its problem and value mechanism
    -> identify the boundary of the fit
    -> test one adjacent segment
    -> compare value, retention, economics, and distribution
    -> expand, adapt, or stop

An adjacent segment may require different positioning, onboarding, workflow, product depth, channel, sales motion, pricing, or service model. Report segment-specific evidence rather than hiding the transition inside company-level averages.

## PMF Treadmill

Fit can weaken when the product enters a new segment, changes its promise, adopts a new channel or price, or when technology, competitors, regulation, and expectations change.

    Established fit
    -> environment or scope changes
    -> old signals weaken or become misleading
    -> product, channel, or model adapts
    -> fit is revalidated

AI-native categories make this especially visible because model capability, cost, interfaces, competitors, and user expectations can change quickly. Shipping speed only helps when it remains connected to customer evidence and retained outcomes.

## Measurement System

Use a small constellation rather than one PMF score.

| Layer | Example measures |
| --- | --- |
| Outcome | Retained value-bearing users or accounts, retained contribution |
| First value | Qualified activation, time to value, successful task or transaction |
| Repetition | Cohort retention, frequency, repeat workflow, repeat transaction |
| Dependence | Must-have response and reasons from qualified users |
| Pull | Direct demand, referrals, organic acquisition, sales pull |
| Economics | Willingness to pay, contribution margin, payback, cost to serve |
| Guardrails | Trust, quality, refunds, support burden, concentration, safety |

The correct definitions depend on product topology and natural frequency. Keep observed behavior separate from predictions, survey statements, and management targets.

## Diagnostic Map

| Observed pattern | Check next | Possible interpretation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strong signup growth, weak retention | Source, promise, activation, use case, cohort age | Distribution is amplifying weak or mismatched value. |
| Strong survey score, weak behavior | Eligibility, sample, response bias, product tenure | Declared dependence is selected or value is not repeatedly reached. |
| Strong retention, weak organic pull | Switching cost, narrow need, collaboration, awareness | Value may be real but not naturally distributive. |
| Strong niche retention, weak expansion | Adjacent problem, role, workflow, category, onboarding | PMF is segment-specific and the expansion assumption is wrong. |
| Strong use, weak economics | Payer, price metric, cost to serve, channel model | Product value exists but the business model is not viable. |
| Revenue grows while active value falls | Contracts, discounting, mix, expansion quality | Commercial output is temporarily disconnected from customer value. |
| Marketplace grows but one side churns | Atomic market, liquidity, quality, incentives, participant economics | Aggregate growth is hiding broken local or cross-side fit. |
| Previously strong cohorts weaken | Product change, competition, technology, expectations, reliability | PMF has degraded and must be re-earned. |

These patterns guide investigation; none establishes causality on its own.

## Maturity Model

| Stage | Characteristics | Next constraint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Interest | Attention, interviews, signups, waitlists, or early revenue | Define the segment, problem, value event, and natural frequency. |
| 2. Initial value | Qualified users reach a meaningful first outcome | Establish repeated behavior and mature cohort evidence. |
| 3. Retained segment | A named segment repeatedly receives value | Reconcile survey, qualitative, economic, and distribution evidence. |
| 4. Viable fit system | Product, market, channel, and model work together | Prove marginal performance and one adjacent expansion path. |
| 5. Adaptive fit | The company detects degradation and revalidates fit across change | Preserve customer understanding while scope and organization scale. |

Maturity is evidence quality and decision readiness, not company age, revenue, headcount, funding, or one threshold.

## Build Sequence

1. Define the customer or participant, use case, problem, alternative, payer, geography, and natural frequency.
2. Specify the first-value and repeated-value events with stable entities, windows, and cohorts.
3. Interview successful, failed, churned, and non-adopting customers to map the mechanism and boundary.
4. Measure qualified activation and mature retention by segment, source, role, and use case.
5. Run the Sean Ellis Test only with qualified users and retain sample, response, and open-text context.
6. Reconcile observed behavior, declared dependence, customer language, organic pull, and contradictory evidence.
7. Test willingness to pay, contribution, cost to serve, payback, and channel-model fit.
8. State the current PMF claim, confidence, boundary, strongest counterevidence, and unresolved risk.
9. Scale one mechanism or adjacent segment at a time and compare marginal quality with the original retained segment.
10. Revalidate when the market, product, channel, price, model, technology, or customer expectation changes.

## PMF Review

A recurring PMF review should answer:

- Which segment and use case is the current claim about?
- What value event repeats, and at what natural frequency?
- Are mature cohorts retaining, expanding, or weakening?
- What do qualified users say they would miss, and why?
- Which evidence contradicts the PMF claim?
- Do price, contribution, payback, and cost to serve remain viable?
- Has a product, market, channel, or model change invalidated earlier evidence?
- What decision follows: deepen, scale, adapt, narrow, or stop?

## Common Mistakes

- Treating a launch spike, waitlist, revenue, investor interest, or social attention as PMF.
- Applying the 40% rule to unqualified or mixed respondents.
- Treating a flattening retention curve as proof of a large or profitable market.
- Averaging strong and weak segments into one score.
- Confusing activation completion with repeated customer value.
- Hiring a growth team to manufacture value the product does not deliver.
- Scaling acquisition before mature retention and economics are understood.
- Assuming fit, once reached, is permanent.

## Evidence and Limits

- PMF is a diagnostic judgment supported by multiple forms of evidence, not a directly observable binary state.
- Survey, retention, market, economic, and distribution evidence can disagree; disagreement should remain visible.
- Thresholds and time horizons depend on product frequency, market, segment, business model, and evidence quality.
- Public company material rarely provides complete cohorts, survey samples, economics, counterfactuals, and failed expansion evidence.
- A company outcome cannot be attributed to one metric, framework, person, or team.
