# Product-Market Fit

> Product-Market Fit is evidence that a defined market repeatedly receives meaningful value from a product under conditions that can support continued use and a viable business. It is not a launch milestone or permanent badge.

**[English](README.md)｜[简体中文](README.zh.md)**

## Definition and Boundaries

PMF connects a specific market, problem, product, and repeated outcome. It is stronger than interest, signup growth, satisfaction, or a single survey score.

| Signal | What it can show | What it cannot prove alone |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Customer interviews | Problem, language, alternatives, perceived value | Actual repeated behavior or market size |
| Sean Ellis Test | Stated must-have dependence in a selected user group | Universal fit or causal retention |
| Activation | Users can reach an early value event | Value continues over the natural cycle |
| Retention cohorts | Repeated behavior persists for a cohort | Healthy economics or broad market demand |
| Organic demand | Users recommend, search for, or return to the product | Whether growth is incremental or durable |
| Willingness to pay | Some users value the outcome economically | Scalable margin, payback, or total market size |

## Fit System

```text
Defined market and problem
-> product promise
-> first value
-> repeated value and retention
-> willingness to adopt or pay
-> repeatable distribution and viable economics
```

PMF should be evaluated by segment. Strong fit in one use case can disappear when averaged with a larger weak-fit audience.

## Four Connected Fits

| Fit | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Market-Product Fit | Does the product solve an important problem for a defined market? |
| Product-Channel Fit | Does the product fit how a channel distributes and converts demand? |
| Channel-Model Fit | Can channel economics support the business model? |
| Model-Market Fit | Can market size, price, and cost support the intended business? |

These fits constrain one another. Product value can be real while distribution or economics remain unworkable.

## Evidence Framework

| Layer | Evidence | Diagnostic question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Problem | Frequency, severity, alternatives, switching behavior | Is the problem important enough to change behavior? |
| Value | Successful value event and qualitative explanation | What outcome do strong users receive? |
| Retention | Mature cohort curve over the natural interval | Does value repeat without constant reacquisition? |
| Segment | Retention and must-have response by use case | Where is fit strongest and weakest? |
| Distribution | Incremental acquisition quality and word of mouth | Can the market be reached repeatedly? |
| Economics | Contribution margin, payback, willingness to pay | Can value support a viable model? |

No universal threshold proves PMF. The often-cited 40% “very disappointed” result from the Sean Ellis Test is a heuristic whose validity depends on respondent selection, timing, sample size, segment, and product frequency.

## PMF Treadmill

Fit can weaken when the product enters a new segment, changes its promise, adopts a new channel or price, or when technology, competitors, and expectations change.

```text
Find fit in a narrow segment
-> grow into adjacent demand
-> detect changed retention or value
-> adapt product, channel, or model
-> revalidate fit
```

AI-native categories make this especially visible because model capability, cost, interfaces, competitors, and user expectations can change quickly.

## Diagnosis

| Pattern | Likely question | Next evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Strong acquisition, weak retention | Promise mismatch or insufficient repeated value | Retention by source, use case, and activation path |
| Strong survey score, weak behavior | Selection bias or stated value not reached | Qualified sample audit and cohort behavior |
| Strong niche retention, weak expansion | Adjacent segments have different problems | Segment interviews and separate cohorts |
| Strong use, weak economics | Price, payer, cost, or channel-model mismatch | Contribution margin and willingness to pay |
| Previously strong cohorts weaken | Product, market, competition, or expectation changed | Cohort comparison and lost-user research |
| Growth depends on discounts or paid acquisition | Weak organic demand or channel fit | Holdouts, payback, post-incentive retention |

## Operating Method

1. Define the market, user, problem, current alternative, and natural usage frequency.
2. Identify the value event and interview users who repeatedly receive that value.
3. Measure activation and mature retention by use case, source, and segment.
4. Use PMF survey evidence as one input, not a pass/fail gate.
5. Test willingness to adopt or pay and reconcile it with service cost and distribution.
6. State confidence and contradictory evidence explicitly.
7. Revalidate whenever the product, segment, channel, price, or market changes.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating a launch spike, waitlist, revenue, or investor interest as PMF.
- Applying the 40% rule to unqualified or mixed respondents.
- Averaging strong and weak segments into one score.
- Hiring a growth team to manufacture value that the product does not deliver.
- Scaling acquisition before mature retention and economics are understood.
- Assuming fit, once reached, is permanent.

## Evidence and Limits

- PMF is a diagnostic judgment supported by multiple forms of evidence, not a directly observable binary state.
- Survey, retention, market, and economic evidence can disagree; disagreement should remain visible.
- Thresholds and time horizons depend on product frequency, market, segment, and business model.
